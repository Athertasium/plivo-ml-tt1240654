The starter's train.py refits on all rows and predicts on those same rows; with 100 turns per language and a 5% budget that is five turns, that number is meaningless and I never tuned against it.

2. Two leaks I refused to use

Both would have scored spectacularly on this data. Both are causality violations, and both are worthless in a live agent.

LeakStrength on this dataWhy it is invalid
Pause duration
pause_end − pause_start		 hold 0.62 s vs eot 1.34 s (English) At the instant a pause starts, how long it will last is the future.
Total file length 100% accurate — every eot pause ends within 0.9 ms of EOF "Is there audio after this pause" is precisely what we are asked to predict.

Rather than just avoiding them, I made compliance auditable. Two independent guards:

  • Column level. dataset.read_pause_rows whitelists turn_id, audio_file, pause_index, pause_start. predict.py cannot see pause_end or label at all, and asserts as much on startup.
  • Sample level. n_causal_frames keeps only frames lying entirely before pause_start, and every normalisation statistic — noise floor, pitch median, mean pause length — is computed inside that prefix. Whole-file statistics would smuggle the future in through the normaliser.

This also drove a design choice: the "how long has this speaker been talking since their last pause" feature is recovered from the energy gate rather than from the previous row's pause_end, so the model needs no annotation at inference time and the whitelist stays narrow.

3. The insight that reframed the problem

Reading score.py closely: a false cutoff is charged only when

p ≥ threshold  AND  delay < pause_duration

So a hold shorter than the action delay is free, however badly it is ranked. Overall AUC is therefore the wrong health metric. Measuring AUC of EOT against only holds longer than D exposed the real failure:

Respectable overall (0.644), at chance on the long holds that actually decide the score (0.513). The model was separating the free ones.

Two changes followed directly from this, and both are the parts of the solution I would defend hardest:

Scorer-matched training costs

Each hold is weighted by the fraction of the scorer's own delay grid at which it would actually be dangerous, so training stops spending capacity on the free short holds. EOT rows keep weight 1. This is a training-time cost, not a feature — pause duration never reaches predict.py.

Spreading scores across the threshold grid

Hindi kept selecting threshold=0.05, the lowest grid value — i.e. "fire on everything", the baseline. That is a calibration symptom, not a ranking one: score.py sweeps a fixed grid and only cares which side of a line a score falls on, so scores bunched into a narrow band leave the sweep no usable operating points. A monotone map through the training-score CDF onto [0.06, 0.97] fixes it. Being monotone it cannot change AUC; it exists purely to give the sweep resolution. The lower bound sits just above the lowest threshold, which guarantees the model can never score worse than the silence-timer baseline.

4. How the score moved

Run 3 regressed: I changed the pitch tracker and added features in the same run, learned nothing from it, and had to unpick them separately. Kept in the log because it is the honest history.

The counter-intuitive one

The starter's autocorrelation pitch tracker is the biased estimator — it tapers with lag and leans toward high F0. Correcting it to unbiased made the pitch histogram look far more physical (English p10 152 → 103 Hz) and made the features worse. Adding proper octave resolution did not recover it either:

Biased (original)0.109kept, on evidence

Best explanation: the taper suppresses spurious long-lag peaks in exactly the low-energy frames before a pause where these features are read — and since every pitch feature is speaker-relative (slope, z-score against the talker's own median), a consistent bias cancels out. A more "correct" signal processing choice was the wrong engineering choice, and only measurement caught it.

5. What the model learned

The signs are linguistically sensible, which is a useful sanity check: pitch dropping toward the bottom of the speaker's own range (f0_last_z negative), energy trailing off (en_last_rel negative), and less voicing at the tail all push toward end-of-turn. prior_sil_max is the feature that came out of error analysis: a talker who has already produced long mid-turn silences makes any given silence weaker evidence that they are finished.

An ablation removing all context features (keeping acoustics only) is worse across the board — 1350 ms vs 1305 ms English OOF — so the context features are earning their place rather than memorising "turns are about 11 seconds".

6. Where it still fails — the honest part

Hindi never beats its 850 ms baseline. The arithmetic explains why. To win at an 800 ms delay the model would need ≥94 of 100 turn-ends to fire (otherwise the 1.6 s timeouts on missed ends swamp the gain) while keeping every firing long hold inside a 5-turn budget. A threshold low enough for 94% recall on turn-ends is far too low to suppress the long holds. An AUC near 0.65 cannot buy both.

Hindi is also intrinsically a harder target than the brief suggests: no Hindi hold pause exceeds 1.5 s, so a plain 850 ms timer already sits inside the budget. Since the hidden test is mostly Hindi, 850 ms — not 1600 ms — is the real bar, and the calibration floor at least guarantees no regression below it.

The residual errors are concentrated in hesitant speakers who pause mid-phrase with level pitch and no final lengthening. That is genuinely ambiguous from prosody alone — a human listener resolves it lexically, from whether the clause is syntactically complete. No amount of pitch and energy engineering recovers that.

7. Why this beats the status quo

  • Against a silence timer (what naive VAD endpointing does): 23% lower response delay on English at the same 5% interruption rate, and never worse on Hindi by construction.
  • Against a leak-fitted model: a model using pause duration or file length would post a near-perfect score here and fail completely in production. Refusing those is the difference between a number and a system.
  • Against in-sample reporting: adding the periodicity feature moved OOF down 1290 → 1228 ms while moving in-sample up 1152 → 1192 ms. The two numbers disagree about the sign of the change, and only one of them is honest — anyone tuning on in-sample is optimising noise on a 5-turn budget.
  • Deployment shape: ships as four arrays in an .npz; inference is a standardise-then-dot-product with no pickle to break across scikit-learn versions. Gradient boosting won three of four ablation columns and was still rejected for this reason, since its edge on the actual metric was nil where it counted.

8. Human vs coding agent

HUMANDirection and judgement calls. Chose the track; approved the plan and the phase ordering; set the constraint that causality compliance had to be auditable rather than merely correct, which drove the whitelist design; made the call to ship robust logistic regression over the better-scoring gradient booster.
AGENTImplementation and measurement. Data inspection that surfaced both leaks; the vectorised FFT pitch/spectral front end replacing the starter's O(n²) per-frame loop; the 21 causal features; the grouped-CV and cross-lingual evaluation harness; the dangerous-hold diagnostic; the scorer-matched cost weights and threshold-grid calibration; the ablation table; and these documents.

The agent's leverage was in mechanical throughput — writing the harness, running ablations, and catching the F0 regression by measurement rather than intuition. The judgement that shaped the outcome was in reading the scorer carefully enough to realise short holds are free, and in refusing two leaks that would have produced a much better number and a much worse system.
